# English Teacher Knowledge Pack

# Correction Method

Use a simple correction pattern: natural version, reason, reusable pattern. Example: "I will join the meeting later" is natural for future participation. "I will attend the meeting later" is also correct but a little more formal.

When the user is practicing speaking, correct the most important issue first: meaning, grammar that blocks understanding, word choice, then style. Do not interrupt fluency with too many details.

# Translation Principles

Good translation preserves intent, tone, and audience. A business message often needs to sound natural, not literal. Ask about audience and formality when it matters.

For Chinese-to-English workplace messages, prefer clear subject-verb-object sentences. Replace indirect or context-heavy phrasing with explicit ownership, timing, and action.

For English-to-Chinese translation, explain idioms, implied tone, and cultural context when a direct translation would sound strange.

# Useful Translation Labels

When offering alternatives, label them by tone:

- Natural: the default, idiomatic version.
- Professional: suitable for email, meetings, and stakeholders.
- Concise: short and direct.
- Polite: softer wording for requests, disagreement, or follow-up.
- Casual: suitable for chat with close coworkers.

# Meeting English: Opening

Common phrases:

- "Let's get started."
- "Thanks everyone for joining."
- "The goal of today's meeting is to align on next steps."
- "Let's quickly go around and share updates."
- "I will keep us on time."
- "Before we dive in, does anyone have anything urgent to add?"

# Meeting English: Clarifying

Common phrases:

- "Could you clarify what you mean by that?"
- "Just to make sure I understand, are you saying that...?"
- "Can you walk us through the main concern?"
- "What is the expected outcome?"
- "Do we have a deadline for this?"
- "Who is the owner for this item?"

# Meeting English: Polite Interruption

Common phrases:

- "Sorry to jump in, but I have one quick question."
- "May I add one point here?"
- "Can I pause you for a second to clarify something?"
- "I want to make sure we do not miss this detail."
- "Before we move on, could we confirm the decision?"

# Meeting English: Disagreeing

Use respectful disagreement:

- "I see your point, but I have a slightly different view."
- "I agree with the goal, but I am concerned about the timeline."
- "That could work. My concern is the risk around..."
- "I am not fully convinced yet. Could we look at the data?"
- "Can we consider another option?"

# Meeting English: Summarizing

Common phrases:

- "Let me summarize what we agreed on."
- "The main takeaway is..."
- "We have three action items."
- "The decision is..."
- "The open question is..."
- "I will send a quick follow-up after the meeting."

# Meeting English: Action Items

Common phrases:

- "I will take the action item to..."
- "Could you own this part?"
- "Let's set the deadline for Friday."
- "Please share the draft before end of day."
- "I will follow up with the notes and next steps."
- "Let's revisit this in the next sync."

# Status Updates

A useful status update structure is: progress, blocker, next step.

Example:

"Quick update: the API integration is mostly done. I am blocked by one auth issue. Next, I will confirm the token flow with the backend team and update the PR."

# Email and Chat Patterns

Request:

"Could you please review this when you have a chance?"

Follow-up:

"Just following up on my previous message. Do you have any updates?"

Clarification:

"Could you help me understand the expected behavior here?"

Soft deadline:

"It would be helpful to get your feedback by Thursday so we can stay on schedule."

Escalation:

"This is becoming time-sensitive. Could we align on the next step today?"

# Politeness and Directness

"Give me the file" may sound too direct. Better options:

- "Could you send me the file?"
- "Would you mind sharing the file?"
- "Please send me the file when you have a chance."

"I do not understand" is acceptable, but in meetings a softer version may work better:

- "Could you explain that another way?"
- "I may be missing some context. Could you clarify?"

# Common Grammar Fixes

Use "discuss something", not "discuss about something."

Use "explain something to someone", not "explain me something."

Use "reply to the email", not "reply the email."

Use "attend a meeting" or "join a meeting", not "join in a meeting" for normal business usage.

Use "on time" for punctuality and "in time" for before it is too late.

Use "by Friday" for deadline before or on Friday, and "on Friday" for an event that happens Friday.

# Common Word Choice

"Efficient" means doing something with little waste. "Effective" means producing the intended result.

"Issue" is often more neutral than "problem" in workplace communication.

"Concern" is softer than "worry" in business communication.

"Align" means reach shared understanding or agreement.

"Follow up" means continue communication after a previous discussion.

# Pronunciation Coaching

When the user asks for pronunciation, use simple phonetic guidance and stress marks. Include common trouble spots for Chinese speakers when relevant, such as final consonants, th sounds, r/l distinction, word stress, and sentence rhythm.

Focus on intelligibility before accent reduction. The goal is clear communication, not sounding like someone else.

# Business Presentation Phrases

Opening:

- "Today I will walk you through..."
- "I will cover three points."
- "Let me start with the background."

Transition:

- "Now let's move to..."
- "This brings us to the next point."
- "The key thing to notice is..."

Closing:

- "To wrap up..."
- "The recommendation is..."
- "I am happy to take questions."

# Common Proverbs and Idioms

"Practice makes perfect" means repeated practice leads to improvement.

"Better late than never" means doing something late is better than not doing it.

"The early bird catches the worm" means people who act early often gain an advantage.

"Actions speak louder than words" means behavior matters more than promises.

"When in Rome, do as the Romans do" means adapt to local customs.

"Do not put all your eggs in one basket" means do not risk everything on one option.

"A blessing in disguise" means something that first seems bad later proves helpful.

"The ball is in your court" means it is your turn to act or decide.

"Cut to the chase" means get to the main point.

"On the same page" means sharing the same understanding.

Teach idioms with caution. In formal or international meetings, plain English is often better than idioms.

# Practice Modes

Role-play mode: simulate a meeting, interview, negotiation, or casual conversation. Give feedback after each turn.

Rewrite mode: improve a message for clarity, tone, and naturalness.

Drill mode: practice one grammar point or phrase pattern with short examples.

Shadowing mode: provide short spoken-style sentences for pronunciation and rhythm practice.

Vocabulary mode: teach words with collocations and example sentences.
